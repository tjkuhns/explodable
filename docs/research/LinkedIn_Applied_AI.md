# Your 0-to-Inbound LinkedIn Playbook for Applied AI

**The thesis: Lead as a technical operator with two shipped artifacts, not as a career-changer learning AI.** The research is unambiguous — hiring managers at Anthropic, OpenAI, Baseten, Decagon, and Sierra pattern-match on *evidence of shipping*, not on career narrative. Your eval writeup and content engine demo are the profile, and everything else (headlines, About, posts) is just distribution. The Customer Success + Lever ATS + buyer psychology background is a legitimate asset **only** when framed as "technical operator who sat in CS," never as "CSM who wants to pivot." That framing distinction will decide whether you land as a credible Applied AI candidate or get filed under the "aspiring" pile that FDE hiring managers actively skip. This document assumes you execute against that positioning.

One macro calibration up front: the realistic timeline from 0-follower LinkedIn to steady inbound is **6–12 months**, per Jason Liu's documented trajectory and Hamel Husain's advice. You can compress that to **8–12 weeks of meaningful conversations** — not job offers — *if* you ship the two artifacts publicly in the first two weeks and pair them with 2–3 posts/week. Do not expect the profile alone to generate inbound. The artifacts generate inbound; the profile converts it.

## The positioning you should own

**Agentic engineering is now a legitimate industry term**, endorsed by Andrej Karpathy, covered by The New Stack (March 2026), institutionalized by the ICSE 2026 AGENT workshop, and adopted by Addy Osmani (Google) in his widely-cited February 2026 essay. Michael Kennedy's canonical framing — *"Vibe coding = YOLO. Agentic engineering = AI does implementation, human owns architecture, quality, correctness"* — is the one you should internalize. Anthropic's Forward Deployed Engineer job description explicitly lists "advanced prompt engineering, agent development, evaluation frameworks, and deployment at scale" as required skills. You can lead with this identity without cringe.

**But there's a failure mode: leading with Claude Code as identity rather than outcome.** "Claude Code power user" as a headline reads hobbyist. "Shipped a content engine with eval harness, built with Claude Code pair programming" reads practitioner. The rule: tool is the *how*, outcome is the *what*, and the *what* always goes first. Hamel Husain mocks this pattern directly on LinkedIn — his "LLM bullshit knife: Agentic → Function calls that work" — and the audience you want to impress nods along with him.

Your actual differentiated specialty — which is stronger than you probably think — is **buyer psychology applied to AI systems for B2B SaaS GTM and CS workflows**. Decagon, Sierra, Harvey, Lorikeet, Intercom, and every mid-stage AI-native selling to enterprise needs someone who understands why pilots stall, how procurement thinks, and what a CS handoff actually looks like — *and* can ship an eval harness for their support agent. That person is rare. That person is you, if you frame it correctly.

## How to write the About section

**Structure: 3 paragraphs, ~1,100–1,400 characters total.** The "see more" fold cuts at roughly the first 220 characters on desktop; that's your hook budget. Eugene Yan's rule — "I title my essays like I name my functions" — applies doubly to the About section. No mission statements. No "passionate about AI." No emoji section dividers.

**Paragraph 1 — the work, right now (the hook):** Lead with what you build and for whom. Example structure: *"I build evaluation harnesses and agent systems for B2B SaaS teams selling to customer success and GTM buyers. Two artifacts worth your time: [1] a domain-specific eval methodology for [specific workflow], and [2] a content engine that ships against [specific constraint]. Both links below."* This is paragraph 1 because the first 220 characters decide whether anyone reads paragraph 2.

**Paragraph 2 — the bridge (your unfair advantage):** This is where buyer psychology + Lever ATS + B2B SaaS becomes a feature. Example: *"Before AI, I spent years inside Employ/Lever as a Customer Success Manager watching B2B SaaS pilots stall, succeed, and die. I learned how procurement thinks, how CS handoffs break, and why buyers say yes in demos and no in implementation. I now apply that pattern recognition to where AI agents actually fail in production — which is rarely the model and almost always the deployment."* This paragraph does the heavy positioning work: it says "I'm not learning customer problems, I already know them" — which is exactly what Anthropic's FDE JD means by *"3+ years in a technical, customer-facing role."*

**Paragraph 3 — stakes and call-to-action:** One line on what you're looking for, one line on what to do. Example: *"Currently open to Applied AI / Forward Deployed / Solutions Architect / DevRel roles at AI-native companies, remote from Youngstown OH. Also taking on a small number of consulting engagements for teams shipping agents into enterprise. DMs open — I respond to anything with a specific technical question attached."* The "specific technical question attached" filter is a credibility signal; Simon Willison uses a version of it.

**How much old career to mention:** Paragraph 2 is the only place. Name Employ/Lever once (searchable, reputable, ATS-domain relevance is legitimate). Never use the phrase "career pivot," "transitioning into AI," "aspiring AI engineer," or "on a journey." SaaStr's Jason Lemkin publicly warns that CSM→FDE conversions are rare; your job is to make yours read as already-completed, not in-progress.

## The Experience section — and the Founder trap

**Do not use "Founder" as your current title.** LinkedIn CEO Ryan Roslansky confirmed in 2025 that "Founder" additions grew 60% YoY and have tripled since 2022. Sophisticated hiring managers now read a solo "Founder" listing as *"between jobs, defaulting to a title."* Anthropic's FDE JD does say *"Former technical founders are also encouraged to apply,"* but this only counts when there's a registered entity, revenue, product, or raised capital behind it.

**Recommended current-role framing (ranked):**

1. **"Independent AI Engineer"** — the Hamel Husain title. Reads as chosen, technical, and present-tense. Pair with a one-line company field like "Independent Practice" or a personal brand name. Most algorithmically and semantically clean.
2. **"AI Engineer, Building [specific thing]"** — e.g., *"AI Engineer — Building eval tooling and content systems for B2B SaaS GTM."* Focused and verifiable.
3. **"Applied AI Engineer (Independent)"** — highest recruiter-search match since "Applied AI" is now a named discipline at OpenAI, Anthropic, and frontier labs. Slightly more aggressive claim; only use if your shipped artifacts support the "applied" framing (yours do).

The Experience description itself should follow Het Trivedi's (Baseten FDE) pattern: concrete technical artifacts with links, specific numbers, no hype. *"Shipped: domain-specific eval harness covering [N] failure modes for [specific workflow], surfaced a [N]% regression in [specific model behavior]. Content engine generating [N artifacts/week] against [specific editorial constraint], built with Claude Code pair programming."*

**How to treat Customer Success at Employ/Lever:** Feature it, don't bury it — but retitle the description, not the title itself. Keep "Customer Success Manager" as the literal title (searchable, verifiable, ATS-standard). In the description, front-load technical and customer-discovery framing: *"Embedded with enterprise buyers of a complex B2B SaaS ATS, diagnosing why pilots stalled and building the workflow integrations that unstuck them. Translated business objections into product requirements. This is the exact skill Forward Deployed Engineers use daily — the Salesforce/a16z 'grandma getting an iPhone' problem."* That last sentence is worth including verbatim — the "grandma/iPhone" metaphor is the canonical FDE leader shorthand for what CS people already do.

**Technical recruiting:** Keep it. One or two bullets, emphasizing technical-domain fluency (you read engineering JDs for a living, which is a real signal). Don't hide it; don't pad it.

## The Featured section is your profile's hook

This is the single most underused real estate on LinkedIn and the highest-leverage element for your specific goal. Four pinned items, in this order:

1. **Your eval methodology writeup**, hosted on your own domain (not Medium — the extra hop kills dwell). Custom thumbnail, not the auto-generated one. Title framed as a function name, Eugene Yan-style: not "How I Think About Evals" but *"Evaluating [specific workflow] agents: error analysis, fail modes, and the flywheel."*
2. **Your content engine demo** — ideally a 60–90 second vertical video with captions, hosted natively on LinkedIn, demonstrating the system running. Video completion rate is a top-5 signal in the current algorithm. Include a frozen first frame with the system visibly operating (not your face talking about it).
3. **A pinned introduction post** — the "first post" described in the content strategy section below. This post exists to give profile visitors something to comment on.
4. **One external credibility anchor** — a GitHub repo, a newsletter signup, or a written piece on a second domain. Prevents the profile from being fully LinkedIn-contained (which reads hobbyist).

Do not pin generic LinkedIn articles, certificates, course completions, or "I'm excited to share" posts. Anthropic's Alex Albert's Featured section is a useful model — his pinned content is all Anthropic product launches and technical writeups, zero meta-content.

## Cadence, format, and timing

**Cadence: 2–3 posts per week. Not daily.** Buffer's 2026 analysis of 4.8 million posts shows 2–5 posts/week adds roughly 1,182 impressions per post over 1/week. Above one post per 24 hours triggers algorithmic self-cannibalization — LinkedIn surfaces only one of your posts to a given follower per day. Hamel Husain, Jason Liu, and Eugene Yan all post **irregularly but consistently** — bursts around artifacts, quiet weeks otherwise. Topic DNA consistency matters far more than frequency.

**Timing: Tuesday/Wednesday/Thursday, 8:00–10:00 AM Eastern** is the current consensus peak for a technical B2B audience. Buffer's 2026 data shows a second peak emerging Wednesday 4 PM and Friday 3–4 PM (post-work scroll). Never post weekends for B2B tech. The first 60–90 minute "golden hour" still matters even under the new 3–8 hour momentum window — if the post earns dwell in the first 90 minutes, the algorithm will continue to distribute it through hour 6.

**Format priority: document carousel > native video > single annotated image > text-only > poll.** Document carousels (PDF format, 5–10 slides, 1080×1350 portrait) hit ~6.6% engagement rate — the highest of any format for technical content — because they dominate dwell time. Use them for eval walkthroughs, RAG architectures, decision rubrics, and "read-the-paper-for-you" breakdowns. Native vertical video with captions grows faster than any other format but requires real production effort; prioritize for the content engine demo specifically. Text posts still win for hot-take/opinion if the hook is strong. Polls are dead (~0.07% engagement, algorithmically demoted).

**Length: 1,200–1,900 characters for long-form text posts.** Under 200 characters rarely gets engagement. Over 2,500 starts hurting dwell completion. Line breaks every 1–3 lines — walls of text fail on mobile (58%+ of traffic).

**Links: take the 60% in-post reach penalty when you have to.** The "link in first comment" trick is now also detected and penalized. The cleanest pattern: post the substance natively, link to the artifact 24 hours later as its own post, or use LinkedIn Newsletters (which get their own distribution path with notification push).

## Building in public — the nuanced verdict

**Still credible in April 2026 if executed at the right abstraction level; dead if executed as daily progress theater.** The distinction matters because 2023–2024 LinkedIn was saturated with "Day 37 of building my AI startup 🚀" posts from founders who weren't building, which poisoned the well for everyone else.

**What works now:** post-mortem building-in-public ("we shipped, here's what broke"); artifact building-in-public (you ship the repo/writeup, the post is just distribution); problem-first narrative ("we had an eval problem, here's the approach, here's the data"). Hamel's Rechat case study and Eugene Yan's entire blog are the model.

**What fails:** daily micro-updates, "just shipped!" with no detail, gratitude-journal process posts, build-log Twitter threads ported to LinkedIn verbatim, and anything with "Day N of..." in the hook. The 2026 audience reads these as marketing-in-public, not building-in-public.

**Your specific call:** Yes, document the engine-building process — but only posts that pass the test *"would a future version of me want to find this post on Google when researching the same problem?"* If the answer is no, don't post it. Share insights from research reports is fine if the insight is *yours* and specific. "Reading [paper] and noticing [specific thing] nobody's talking about" works. "Here's what I'm learning" signals student.

## What kills credibility — the avoid list

**Phrases that trigger instant "this person doesn't build" with AI practitioners:**
"Game changer." "Let's unpack this." "Here's what nobody tells you about." "This will blow your mind." "AI will change everything." "Prompt engineering is the new programming." "It's not just X, it's Y." (ChatGPT-4's signature cadence — instantly flags AI-written prose.) "I've been thinking a lot about…" "Mark my words / screenshot this." "In the ever-evolving landscape of AI." Overuse of *delve, leverage, robust, seamless, transformative, unlock, empower, elevate, paradigm shift, cutting-edge.*

**Visual tells of "aspiring" not practitioner:**
🚀 rocket emojis — Bloomberg's January 2026 piece identified this as the single biggest credibility kill. Green ✅ checkmark bullet lists. 🔥/💡/🎯 as section dividers. All-caps hooks ("STOP DOING THIS."). "1/ 2/ 3/" Twitter thread formatting on LinkedIn. Numbered "7 lessons I learned" listicles. Stock hero images of men in suits / glowing brains / neural-net meshes. "Follow me for more AI insights" footers. Acronym-stuffed headlines.

**Content patterns that scream aspiring:**
Recycled launch news with no original take ("GPT-X just dropped, here's why it matters: [generic summary]"). Screenshots of ChatGPT conversations as the entire post. "I asked ChatGPT [X] and the answer will shock you." Listicles of 30 AI tools you haven't used. Predictions without a model or stake. Taking credit for repos/papers without adding signal. Poll posts asking "What do you think?" Engagement-bait CTAs ("Comment YES below"). The AI-detection models inside 360Brew reportedly demote posts that read as AI-written; per Bloomberg's 2026 survey of 200 LinkedIn posts across 50 profiles, ~75% looked AI-generated to a trained reader — and lost credibility accordingly. Write in your own voice, even if it's rougher.

**The "aspiring vs. practitioner" test — apply to every post and profile element:**

| Signals "aspiring" | Signals "practitioner" |
|---|---|
| "I'm learning about evals" | "Here's the eval harness I shipped, here's what it surfaced" |
| "Thoughts on the future of agents?" | "We switched from X to Y, recall went from 0.62 to 0.78, latency doubled" |
| "Excited to share my first AI project" | "Shipped [specific artifact]. [Specific failure mode] was the hard part" |
| "Following the AI space closely" | "Disagree with [specific person] on [specific claim] — here's why" |
| Tools in headline as identity | Tools named in-post as implementation detail |
| "Pivoting into AI" | "I build [specific thing] for [specific buyer]" |

Every profile element and every post must pass this test. This is the single most important calibration in this whole document.
