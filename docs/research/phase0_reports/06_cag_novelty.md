# CAG for essay generation is genuinely uncharted territory

**No one has publicly evaluated Cache-Augmented Generation — or any full-knowledge-base-in-context approach — for multi-source argumentative essay generation.** The gap is not subtle. Every CAG and long-context-vs.-RAG paper evaluates exclusively on QA benchmarks (SQuAD, HotPotQA, NarrativeQA). Every argument-generation paper uses retrieval pipelines, not context stuffing. The specific task described — loading ~300 structured findings into context and asking a model to select, sequence, and synthesize 6–8 into a coherent analytical essay — sits in a genuine blind spot between two well-studied areas that have never been connected.

## The prior art stops at question answering

Chan et al.'s CAG paper (arXiv:2412.15605) tests only Llama 3.1 8B on SQuAD and HotPotQA with at most **85K tokens** of context. It does not mention generation tasks. The broader RAG-vs-long-context literature — Zhu et al. (EMNLP Industry 2024, arXiv:2407.16833), Li et al. (arXiv:2501.01880), Yu et al. ("In Defense of RAG," arXiv:2409.01666) — also evaluates exclusively on QA. On the argument-generation side, Hua & Wang (ACL 2018, aclanthology.org/P18-1021) and ConQRet (NAACL 2025, aclanthology.org/2025.naacl-long.293) build retrieval-augmented argument systems, never testing what happens when you skip retrieval and stuff everything into context. The closest adjacent work is PESA (arXiv:2410.22642), which generates argumentative essays but from parametric knowledge alone, not from external sources. Anthropic's own prompt-caching documentation (docs.anthropic.com/en/docs/build-with-claude/prompt-caching) lists "include a full knowledge base" as a use case but provides no evaluation data and explicitly warns that "more context isn't automatically better."

## Generation failure modes differ fundamentally from QA failures

The lost-in-the-middle effect (Liu et al., TACL 2024, arxiv.org/abs/2307.03172) establishes a robust U-shaped positional bias — models attend best to the beginning and end of context, worst to the middle — but this was measured on retrieval tasks only. For generation, the evidence base is thinner but points to **distinct and arguably worse** failure modes. LongGenBench (Wu et al., ICLR 2025, arxiv.org/abs/2409.02076) found that models scoring well on retrieval benchmarks like RULER still failed at long-form generation, with a Pearson correlation of only **0.51** between retrieval and generation performance at 16K tokens. Generation-specific failures include instruction drift (models deviate from the original plan as output length grows), content homogenization (outputs converge toward repetitive patterns), and memory retention failure. Separately, Roig (arXiv:2603.08274) showed fabrication rates tripling from **1.19%** at 32K tokens to floors above **10%** at 200K tokens in QA — but no one has measured fabrication rates during extended generation under high context loads. The widely referenced "effective context" threshold converges around **40–50%** of the advertised context window (Veseli et al., COLM 2025, arxiv.org/abs/2508.07479; the StRing paper, arXiv:2410.18745). No validated "2.5x rule" exists in the literature.

## Source selection is positionally biased, but nobody has tested the "pick 6 from 300" scenario

Ravaut et al. (ACL 2024, arxiv.org/abs/2310.10570) is the single most relevant paper here: they demonstrated pronounced **lead bias** in summarization, where LLMs disproportionately draw from the first and last sections of source text, with middle content systematically under-represented. A companion paper on positional bias and faithfulness (arXiv:2410.23609) found models **hallucinate more for middle-positioned documents**. Mechanistically, Wu et al. (ICLR 2025, arxiv.org/abs/2404.15574) identified that fewer than **5% of attention heads** handle retrieval, and these heads have inherent positional preferences rooted in causal attention masks and RoPE encodings. However, the specific scenario of instructing a model to select a subset from hundreds of available sources for open-ended generation has never been studied. Whether selection is purely positional, salience-weighted, or topic-proximity-driven remains an open empirical question.

## Utilization metrics exist for QA but not for selective generation

Several measurement frameworks are available but none are purpose-built for this use case. ALCE (Gao et al., EMNLP 2023, arxiv.org/abs/2305.14627) introduced citation precision and recall for generated text — measuring whether cited sources actually support claims. ETHIC (arXiv:2410.16848) proposed Information Coverage, measuring what proportion of input context is required to answer a query. HELMET (Yen et al., ICLR 2025, arxiv.org/abs/2410.02694) includes generation-with-citations as one of seven evaluation categories. Ravaut et al.'s approach of aligning generated sentences to source sentences via ROUGE-1 F1 and tracking positional distribution provides a practical template. But no existing metric directly measures **diversity of source selection** — whether a model draws from documents at all positions versus clustering at the beginning — in the "select K from N for generation" paradigm.

## A publishable contribution requires specific experiments

This is a **genuinely novel** application. The novelty sits at the intersection of three well-established but disconnected research threads: CAG/long-context evaluation, positional bias, and knowledge-grounded generation. A credible arXiv preprint would need four components. First, a **CAG vs. RAG comparison** on argumentative essay generation with human and automated quality evaluation (coherence, argument structure, factual grounding). Second, **positional bias measurement** — randomize document order across runs, track which findings get selected, and plot selection probability by position. Third, **source coverage and diversity metrics** — adapt ALCE citation tracking and Ravaut et al.'s alignment method to measure KB utilization breadth. Fourth, **ablation on KB size** — test whether the failure modes observed at 300 findings differ from 50 or 100. Essential citations: Chan et al. (CAG), Liu et al. (lost-in-the-middle), Ravaut et al. (context utilization in generation), LongGenBench, ALCE, Hua & Wang (argument generation). The contribution differentiates from Chan et al. by extending CAG evaluation from extraction to synthesis, and from Hua & Wang by replacing retrieval with context-stuffing.

This is too substantial for a cookbook or blog post — the experimental design demands a **workshop or short paper** at minimum, with a full paper viable if results reveal novel failure modes. On the novelty spectrum, this sits firmly at **"genuinely novel application with clear research gap"** — not a new method, but the first empirical evaluation of an existing method on a meaningfully different task class where existing results do not transfer.
