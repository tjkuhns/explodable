# From pipeline output to publication, by design

**The evidence converges on a single recommendation: abandon binary approve/reject with full regeneration.** No mature workflow in the peer-reviewed HCI literature or in practitioner accounts from Every.to, Dan Shipper, or Kenny Kane uses that loop, and machine-translation post-editing research (Koponen 2016; Moorkens & O'Brien 2015) identifies it as the worst-documented UX: repeated middling regenerations produce the lowest editor satisfaction. The optimal architecture is a **four-stage pipeline** — targeted section-level revision, a two-pass quality gate built around read-aloud with text-to-speech, a complementary human checklist aimed at the ten dimensions your LLM judge is systematically weak on, and a recurrence-gated editorial decision log that compounds into a living style guide and feeds back into your prompts. The rest of this report is the evidence for each piece and how they fit together for an 8–10 essays plus 20 posts monthly cadence. Numbers that look precise (40% time savings, 65%→0% rulings, 23-minute recovery) are flagged where the underlying evidence has been contested.

## What the research actually says about AI-draft human-edit workflows

The foundational empirical result is **Noy and Zhang (Science 2023)**: 453 professionals writing 20–30 minute business documents saw task time drop 40% and blinded quality rise 18% (0.4 SD) with ChatGPT. Crucially, the time didn't shrink uniformly — it shifted from drafting toward editing and ideation, which is exactly the regime you're already in. Brynjolfsson, Li and Raymond's 2023 NBER study of 5,000 support agents found a +13.8% throughput gain concentrated in the bottom quintile (+35%) with near-zero gain at the top. **AI compresses the skill distribution by lifting mediocre baselines, not by elevating expert ceilings.** Your human editing time is where the ceiling lives.

The HCI literature documents several workflow patterns but no clean winner. **Lee, Liang and Yang's CoAuthor (CHI 2022)** captured 1,445 GPT-3-assisted sessions and found suggestion-based turn-taking — writer drafts, requests continuation, accepts-dismisses-edits — with productivity correlating with suggestion acceptance (r≈0.48). **Yuan, Coenen, Reif and Ippolito's Wordcraft (IUI 2022)** found professional writers used selection-and-command at paragraph granularity, rarely accepting prose verbatim. **Chakrabarty et al. (C&C 2024)** grounded a three-surface interface in Flower and Hayes's plan-translate-review model with 30 writers including MFA students; writers rated AI most useful in translation and review, least useful in planning. The consistent finding across these studies is that **professionals use AI asymmetrically**: heavy for brainstorming, translation, and critique; light for final prose.

Machine translation post-editing is the most mature analogue and delivers quantitative anchors. **Krings's (2001) tripartite effort model** — temporal, technical, cognitive — shows that cognitive effort is often decoupled from edit count: a few high-effort catches can dominate. **Koponen (2016)** documents that below a quality threshold, editing is slower than writing from scratch. **Light post-editing runs 600–1000 words/hour; full post-editing runs 300–400 words/hour** (TAUS, ISO 18587:2017). For a 1000-word AI essay at full-polish quality, budget 2.5–3.5 hours of human editing to hit publication standard; at light polish, 60–100 minutes. Voice-dilution and homogenization are the documented cost: **Padmakumar and He (ICLR 2024)** show instruction-tuned models reduce lexical and content diversity at corpus level (base GPT-3 did not); **Agarwal et al. (Cornell 2024)** find AI nudges non-Western writers toward Western references; **Draxler et al. (TOCHI 2024)** and **Hwang et al. (CSCW 2025)** document that ownership perception scales with edit depth — the workflows that preserve voice are slower, the ones that maximize throughput homogenize. The practical implication: your own style-guide artifacts and rough-up passes are the main defense against voice decay over 100+ pieces.

## Stop regenerating whole drafts

For the 80%-good-one-weak-section case, the evidence-ranked sequence is **(b) targeted AI rewrite > (a) manual edit > (d) three variants > (c) full regeneration**, with the right choice depending on why the section is weak.

**CoEdIT (Raheja et al., EMNLP 2023)** — a 770M-parameter model trained on 82K instruction-edit pairs — was preferred by writers over GPT-3-Edit in head-to-head evaluation and handled composite instructions like "make this more neutral AND simpler." **PEER (Schick et al., 2022)** achieved 49.3 SARI on Wikinews edits versus 32.8 unrevised and beat InstructGPT's 39.4. **IteraTeR and DElIteraTeR (Du, Raheja, Kim et al., ACL/EMNLP 2022)** showed intention-conditioned span-level revision outperforms ungrounded regeneration. The converging finding: **when you can articulate the fix, a specific edit instruction on the existing draft produces writer-preferred output that preserves voice**. **Chakrabarty et al. (2024, "Can AI writing be salvaged?")** identify paragraph-level as the right granularity — small enough to preserve intent, large enough to capture discourse coherence.

Self-refine approaches are narrower than the marketing suggests. **Madaan et al. (NeurIPS 2023)** reported ~20% absolute gain across seven tasks when feedback included localization plus explicit improvement instruction. But **Huang et al. (ICLR 2024)** — "LLMs Cannot Self-Correct Reasoning Yet" — found unsupervised self-correction often degrades reasoning; the gains live in style and safety, not argument. **If the weak section has a factual or analytical problem, you must diagnose it yourself; AI can't self-rescue the thinking.**

Best-of-N is powerful but plateaus fast. **Snell et al. (2024, "Scaling LLM Test-Time Compute Optimally")** found sequential revisions beat parallel best-of-N on easier prompts, with a compute-optimal mix ~4× more efficient than naive BoN. **BoNBoN (NeurIPS 2024) and RBoN (NAACL 2025)** document reward hacking at N>8-16: the selector starts optimizing proxy attributes like length. For essay prose, **N=3 captures most of the quality gain**; N>5 crosses into diminishing returns plus your own selection fatigue.

Here's the decision heuristic, anchored to why the section is weak:

| Cause | First move | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Factual or analytical error | Manual edit (a) | Self-correction literature says AI won't fix reasoning without external diagnosis |
| Stylistic — tone, specificity, transitions, pacing | Targeted AI rewrite with specific instruction (b), always pasting surrounding paragraphs | CoEdIT/PEER evidence; paragraph-level granularity preserves voice |
| "Something's off, I can't name it" | N=3 variants with different angles (d) | Snell sweet spot; forces you to articulate by comparing |
| Framing, audience, or structure is wrong | Full regeneration with modified prompt (c) | Only case where the 80% wasn't actually good |

The **anti-pattern to burn out of the workflow**: binary reject-and-regen. It loses the 80% that worked, introduces new hallucinations, and is the single worst UX in the post-editing literature.

## The uncanny valley has specific coordinates

Knowing precisely where AI writing is thin lets you spend editing minutes at the highest-leverage points. The diagnostic markers are both lexical (cheap to find-and-delete) and structural (the load-bearing edits).

**Lexical tells** from Originality.ai's 10.78M-word ChatGPT corpus analysis and stylometric research: verbs *delve, leverage, utilize, harness, navigate, foster, unlock, elevate, captivate*; nouns *tapestry, landscape, realm, testament, journey, synergy, underpinnings, nuance*; adjectives *crucial, vital, pivotal, robust, seamless, multifaceted, intricate, meticulous, profound*; transitions *furthermore, moreover, notably, ultimately*. Phrase tells: *"It's important to note," "It's worth noting," "In today's fast-paced world," "In the realm of," "When it comes to," "A testament to," "Not only X but also Y,"* and any closing paragraph that begins with *"Ultimately"* or *"In conclusion."*

**Structural tells** are more durable as models evolve. **Muñoz-Ortiz et al. (2024)** found LLMs produce a tight 10–30 token sentence distribution; humans show much wider range. GPTZero's burstiness metric operationalizes this. **Kostka (Frontiers in Education, 2024)** found GPT essays contain significantly more hedges and fewer boosters than student essays, and use active voice almost exclusively where students use both strategically. Add: uniform topic-sentence-three-supports-summary paragraph template; balanced "on-one-hand/on-the-other" conclusions that refuse a position; em-dash abuse; unprompted bolding and bullets; rule-of-three lists in every paragraph.

What editors say AI **actually** lacks, organized by category and backed by **Chakrabarty et al.'s TTCW (CHI 2024)** result — human stories pass 84.7% of creativity tests, LLM stories pass 9–30%:

**Specificity** is the highest-ROI repair. Roy Peter Clark's rule ("get the name of the dog") maps directly: for every abstraction, substitute one concrete noun with a number, date, or proper name. Replace "productivity gains" with "cut revision cycles from three rounds to one." Aim for one proper noun per paragraph. **Voice** is Henrik Karlsson's territory — rewrite one paragraph as though texting a friend, keep that register. **Surprise** is Chakrabarty's originality gap: force one analogy from a domain the piece is not about. **Earned insight** means identifying the sentence where you risk being wrong; if none exists, add one. Convert hedged ("can be considered") to committed ("is"). **Sensory detail**: no emotional abstraction without a physiological correlate. **Rhythm**: plant a three-word sentence, then a 40+ word one. **Stakes**: if you can't finish the sentence "a reader closes this piece different from how they opened it because ___," the ending isn't doing work.

The time-stratified edit protocol: **five minutes on the lexical find-and-delete pass; fifteen minutes on structural sweeps** (sentence-length variance, opening and closing paragraphs, one specificity pass per paragraph); **then the work only humans can do** — one lived detail, one non-obvious analogy, a committed position. The first two tiers are mostly mechanical; the third is where your minutes per word justify themselves.

## A two-pass quality gate, with and without your own voice

Read-aloud has the strongest empirical backing of any single editorial technique, and it's underused because people intuit it won't help. **Cushing and Bodner (2022, Journal of Applied Research in Memory and Cognition)** — the most direct study — found reading aloud significantly improved detection of both typos and contextual errors versus silent reading; participants did not predict the benefit even after performing better. **Baddeley et al. (1981) and Kleiman (1975)** show that suppressing subvocalization specifically impairs semantic and word-order error detection. **Fodor's implicit-prosody research** establishes the mechanism: silent reading generates an implicit prosodic contour that drives parsing; reading aloud externalizes and exposes mismatches. **Pilotti and Chodorow (2012)** show familiarity with your own text reduces silent error detection — reading aloud counteracts this directly.

Text-to-speech-while-following is the empirical peer. **Raskind and Higgins (1995, Learning Disability Quarterly)** found TTS with highlighted follow-along produced the highest total error detection, beating both human read-aloud and unaided silent reading. The catch: **a human voice outperformed TTS on grammar-mechanical errors** because prosody surfaces syntax issues that flat TTS smooths over. The obvious synthesis is a two-pass protocol, which triangulates the strengths.

After letting the draft cool at least overnight (addressed below), **Pass 1 runs neural TTS at 1.2–1.3× with eye-tracking of the text for 5–7 minutes**. This catches typos, wrong-word substitutions, missing and duplicated words, and homophone near-misses — mechanical issues where the eye-ear mismatch earns its keep. Pause and mark the moment they diverge. **Pass 2 is manual read-aloud, forward, at natural pace, pen in hand, for 6–8 minutes.** This catches rhythm problems, sentence-length monotony (the "Provost problem" that dominates AI output), tonal mismatches, run-ons, weak transitions. This is where Elbow, Klinkenborg, Dreyer, Zinsser and the Flaubertian gueuloir do their work — unanimous craft consensus with real cognitive-science support beneath.

**Skip Sans Forgetica and disfluent fonts** — Cushing and Bodner showed no benefit, possible harm. Skip TTS alone without text-following; you lose the mismatch-catch. Record-and-listen adds value for high-stakes pieces only (an extra 10 minutes); empirical support is zero but the theoretical case is clean because it maximally separates writer from reader. For 8–10 long-form pieces monthly, build Passes 1 and 2 into the standard protocol; reserve record-and-listen for the two or three pieces of the month that will actually be scrutinized.

## Editorial cadence for the 30-piece month

Decision fatigue is real but over-hyped. The famous **Danziger, Levav and Avnaim-Pesso (PNAS 2011)** Israeli parole study — favorable rulings dropping from 65% to near zero before meals — has been seriously challenged. **Weinshall-Margel and Shapard (2011)** showed case ordering wasn't random; unrepresented prisoners went last. **Glöckner (2016)** demonstrated the pattern can emerge as a statistical artifact when favorable rulings take longer and judges rationally end sessions rather than start long cases before breaks. **Don't cite the 65%→0% swing as established.** Ego depletion more broadly didn't survive replication: **Hagger et al.'s 2016 pre-registered 23-lab replication (N=2,141) found d=0.04**.

The **robust** evidence sits elsewhere. **Baer and Schnall (2021, Royal Society Open Science)** — credit officers with electronically randomized case order — cleanly demonstrated decision fatigue. **Linder et al. (JAMA Internal Medicine 2014)** show inappropriate antibiotic prescribing rises within clinic sessions. **Stewart et al. (2012)** — directly relevant — found academic journal editors' rejection rates rise and deferrals increase with larger review batches. Rater-drift research is the best-replicated analogue: **Congdon and McQueen (2000)** found 9 of 10 raters became more severe across seven days of scoring 8,285 essays; **Myford and Wolfe (2009)** documented significant drift across 101 raters; ETS uses double-scoring precisely because individual rater consistency degrades within sessions. **Sequential assimilation effects** (Attali; Leckie and Baird) show piece N's rating is contaminated by piece N−1. **Sophie Leroy's attention residue research (2009)** adds the mechanism: switching between similar evaluative tasks leaves cognition stuck on the prior item; explicit closure rituals reduce it.

Translated into a concrete cadence: **never review same-day as generation** (minimum overnight; 24–72 hours preferred). Stephen King's drawer method is craft consensus, not RCT, but convergence is strong. **Cap long-form sessions at two to three pieces, ~90 minutes total**, morning when possible. **Short posts batch separately** in sessions of 5–7, never intermixed with long-form (context-switching cost). **Never exceed 10 total decisions in one sitting.** Use a two-pass structure across different days for long-form: Pass A for structural/argument, Pass B for line-level and rhythm. Jot a one-sentence closure note between pieces (Leroy's finding). The 90-minute ultradian and 23-minute interruption-recovery numbers are directionally right but popularized beyond their direct evidence — use them as defaults, not laws.

For your volumes: four or five long-form review sessions per month (roughly twice weekly, two pieces each, 90 minutes); two short-post review sessions per week (5–7 posts each, 45–60 minutes). This keeps every piece ≥24 hours cold, keeps each session inside the drift envelope, and honors batching-by-type.

## Build your human checklist where the LLM judge is structurally blind

Your ρ=0.841 against an editorial panel is strong — roughly the human-human agreement ceiling reported by **Zheng et al. (2023, MT-Bench)** — but the remaining variance is not randomly distributed. LLM judges have **documented, directional failure modes** that concentrate disagreement on specific dimensions. Your human checklist should target those and ignore dimensions the judge already nails.

**Trust the judge on**: grammar and mechanics, sentence-level fluency, local coherence, conciseness, on-topic compliance, tone classification. **Chakrabarty et al. 2024's TTCW** shows LLM judges reach 0.78+ accuracy on understandability and coherence; **G-Eval (Liu et al. 2023)** hits Spearman 0.514 on summarization fluency. Auditing these is duplicative.

**Distrust the judge on**: originality, humor, factual specificity at edges, argumentative soundness, voice authenticity, cultural specificity, stakes, specificity of examples, rhetorical effectiveness. **Chakrabarty (TTCW CHI 2024)**: "none of the LLMs positively correlate with expert assessments" on creative writing tests. **Dahl et al. (Journal of Legal Analysis 2024)**: LLMs hallucinate 58–88% of verifiable case details. **Panickssery, Bowman and Feng (NeurIPS 2024)**: LLMs favor their own generations, linearly correlated with self-recognition — GPT-4 prefers itself ~10%, Claude-v1 ~25%. The implication for your stack is sharp: **your LLM judge is systematically overrating AI-default-voice prose and underrating idiosyncratic human voice by 10–25%.** **Koo et al. (CoBBLEr, ACL Findings 2024)** document six biases with only 49.6% rank-order agreement with humans; **Stureborg et al. (2024)** confirm run-to-run inconsistency at temperature>0. **Ye et al.'s CALM framework (2024)** documents sycophancy dropping debunking by up to 15% under confident framing.

The checklist below deliberately targets these blind spots. Run it after the LLM judge, not before, so the score doesn't prime you:

1. **Originality of core frame** — has a well-read peer seen this exact framing?
2. **Concrete specificity test** — can any sentence be replaced with "…and similar generalities" without loss?
3. **Load-bearing fact verification** — check the three claims the argument hinges on against primary sources.
4. **Steelman the opposite view** — one sentence; does the piece acknowledge it?
5. **Voice fingerprint** — does the opening sound like you or like polished generic prose?
6. **Surprise audit** — one moment of wit, wordplay, or subverted expectation.
7. **Stakes question** — why does a reader close this different from how they opened it?
8. **Cultural specificity** — references fit the audience, or default to generic Anglophone baseline?
9. **Unstated assumptions** — list two or three undefended premises; willing to defend each?
10. **Cut the weakest 20%** — if you removed it, would the piece be stronger?

For the LLM judge itself, **mitigate known biases with cheap interventions**: temperature=0 plus an average of 3–5 runs on high-stakes dimensions; rotate rubric criterion order across runs; don't use the same model that drafted the text to judge it (self-preference bias); anonymize any identifying markers; strip confident hedges before scoring (sycophancy mitigation).

## Version control that compounds over 100 pieces

Professional editors distinguish between **style guide** (a reference — Chicago Manual, AP, New Yorker house style) and **style sheet** (a project-specific, living document created while editing that records decisions the guide doesn't cover). Einsohn and Schwartz's *Copyeditor's Handbook* treats the per-piece style sheet as mandatory infrastructure. The Guardian style guide — per Marsh's interviews and the engineering blog describing *Typerighter* — is source-controlled, updated continuously, with two or three big revisions a year batched and announced. Datadog and GitLab use Vale with staged rule promotion: suggestion → warning → error. **Dreyer's English** itself began as a ~20-page internal memo that grew by accumulation, then pruning. This is the canonical pattern: surface issue → debate → document → codify → enforce via tooling.

The theoretical basis is **Nonaka and Takeuchi's SECI model**: knowledge creation spirals through externalization (tacit→explicit) → combination → internalization. The bottleneck for a solo operator is **externalization** — forcing tacit editorial instinct into written rules. Decision journaling (Shane Parrish's templates, Annie Duke's framework) is the same move in a different vocabulary: log situation, options, decision, rationale, expected outcome; review at 1-week and 6-month intervals. For AI-era writing, the prompt-engineering literature shows this loop closes when failures flow back into the system: **Anthropic's context-engineering guidance** emphasizes "diverse, canonical examples" over laundry-lists; Braintrust, Maxim and AWS Prescriptive Guidance describe live requests revealing weaknesses being added to golden datasets as the core compounding mechanism.

Build a **four-layer system**. Layer 1 is a lightweight per-piece style sheet — one Markdown file per piece capturing decisions specific to it. Layer 2 is the compounding **Edit Decision Log** — a single Airtable or Obsidian-Dataview database, one row per non-trivial edit, with fields: id, date, piece, before, after, category (diction/rhythm/structure/voice/accuracy/mechanics), rule (one-sentence generalization), reason, severity (house-style vs. voice vs. preference), source (self/editor/reader/AI flag), status (one-off/candidate/rule), recurrence count, expected reader effect. Layer 3 is the consolidated **Style Guide** — a single Markdown file plus Vale configuration, git-versioned. Use recurrence-gated promotion borrowed from GitLab: one-off stays in the log; a candidate (≥3 occurrences across different pieces) gets promoted to Vale warning-level; a rule (candidate surviving two monthly reviews) gets promoted to Vale error-level and added as a canonical few-shot example. Layer 4 is the **AI-facing asset bundle** derived from Layers 2 and 3: a distilled system prompt rubric (≤1,500 tokens, Anthropic-style priority-ordered), a 6–12 few-shot example bank (before/after/rule triplets from the highest-recurrence entries, rotated by task), and optionally a RAG index over the full decision log for inference-time retrieval.

The **operating cadence**: daily, append one row per real edit (seconds). Weekly, cluster and merge duplicates (15 minutes). **Monthly, the compounding step (60–90 minutes)** — read log end-to-end, promote candidates, update the Markdown guide and Vale config (git commit), regenerate the AI rubric and few-shot bank, run a small eval (take three pieces, compare old-prompt vs. new-prompt edits, check for regressions). Quarterly, do Franklin's move: take an admired essay by a target author, run it through your prompt, compare to original; gaps reveal missing rules.

**The discipline that separates compounding from noise is recurrence-gated promotion plus pruning.** Over 100 pieces × 10 edits each = 1,000 rows, but perhaps only 40–80 rules survive to the guide. Archive superseded rules; don't let the guide accumulate. Separate mechanics from voice: Vale handles deterministic linting pre-draft; LLM prompts and RAG handle judgment post-draft. Do not try to lint voice — the signal-to-noise ratio collapses.

The minimal viable stack for a solo operator: **Obsidian or Notion for the Markdown vault**, Dataview plugin for log queries; **Git for version control** of the style-guide directory; **Vale** for codified mechanical rules (start from Google or Microsoft packages, extend personally, promote confidence gradually); **your AI assistant** consuming the three derived assets. Add Airtable if the log exceeds 500 rows, LlamaIndex or Obsidian Smart Connections once you hit 100+ decisions, PerfectIt for per-manuscript consistency scans. Enterprise prompt-versioning tools (Braintrust, Humanloop, Maxim) are overkill; Git tags on the prompt file plus a 5–10 piece eval set is sufficient until you're shipping prompts to external users.

## The integrated architecture

Put the pieces in order. **Generation** runs through the pipeline you already have, constrained by the evolving system prompt plus few-shot bank and (optionally) RAG retrieval from the decision log. **The LLM judge and AI critic** run next, producing scores and critique — but those outputs are now *inputs to your process, not gates*. You ignore their scores on the six dimensions they score reliably (mechanics, fluency, local coherence, conciseness, on-topic, tone) and treat the rest as noisy signal. **The draft cools at least overnight, ideally 24–72 hours.**

Then **you run the section-diagnostic pass with the decision heuristic**: identify the weak section(s), classify cause (factual / stylistic / unarticulable / structural), and apply the matching treatment — manual edit, targeted AI rewrite with surrounding paragraphs as context, N=3 variants, or full regeneration — with targeted rewrite as the default. Next **the lexical find-and-delete sweep** (5 minutes) strips the known AI tells. Then the **structural pass** (15 minutes) handles sentence-length variance, specificity substitutions, opening and closing paragraphs. Then **the high-leverage human pass** (15+ minutes) adds one lived detail, one non-obvious analogy, one committed position. **The two-pass read-aloud gate** — TTS-with-follow first, manual read-aloud second — runs last. Every meaningful edit made along the way gets one row in the decision log.

**Cadence**: review sessions are separated from drafting by ≥24 hours; two to three long-form pieces per 90-minute morning session; 5–7 short posts per separate session; never more than 10 decisions in one sitting. Monthly consolidation promotes candidate rules to the style guide and regenerates AI assets.

**What you gain over binary approve/reject**: the 80% that was already good is preserved (saving roughly 20–30 minutes per reject on a 1000-word piece); your edits accumulate rather than evaporate; your prompts and rubric sharpen against the same failures your human judgment is already catching; and the LLM judge's blind spots — originality, specificity, voice, stakes — get explicit human attention rather than being laundered by a high aggregate score. At 8–10 long-form and 20 short pieces per month, this architecture is the difference between 30 disposable drafts and a compounding editorial practice.

## Conclusion

Three calibrations matter most. **First, the bottleneck isn't your pipeline, it's that your process treats every piece as independent**; version-control for editorial decisions turns every edit into training data for the next piece, which is the only mechanism by which solo output improves over 100+ pieces. **Second, the LLM judge and the human editor are not redundant reviewers — they're specialists with asymmetric blind spots**, and a 10-item human checklist built against the known failure modes (self-preference, verbosity, sycophancy, originality blindness) adds orthogonal signal rather than duplicating work. **Third, the highest-leverage editing moves — specificity substitutions, committed positions, lived detail, rhythm breaks, read-aloud rhythm checks — are also the ones AI can't do, and they compound into voice**, which is the one asset that doesn't homogenize as LLMs improve. The cost of this architecture is a disciplined monthly consolidation habit; the payoff is that piece number 100 is meaningfully better than piece number 10, which is not guaranteed in any regenerate-and-approve workflow.
