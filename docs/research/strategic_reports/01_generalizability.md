# Per-stage retrieval architectures: the evidence generalizes

The hypothesis — that different pipeline stages require different knowledge-access architectures based on cognitive task type — is **strongly supported across all five domains examined**, with legal AI providing the most damning empirical case against monolithic RAG. The Stanford RegLab Magesh et al. study (2024–2025) documents **hallucination rates of 17–33% on flagship commercial RAG-grounded legal tools** (Lexis+ AI, Westlaw AI, Ask Practical Law), and a controlled 2026 benchmark (LaborBench/STARA) shows a **structured statutory-retrieval pipeline beats Westlaw AI 92% vs 58% on multi-jurisdictional surveys** — direct evidence that architecture choice dominates model choice. Finance offers parallel evidence: FinanceBench reports **GPT-4-Turbo + RAG is incorrect or refuses on 81% of questions**, and HybridRAG (BlackRock/NVIDIA) demonstrates GraphRAG and VectorRAG win opposite question types on the same corpus. Medical RAG shows retriever × task interactions of up to 30 points on identical questions. Production software-engineering tools (Cody, Cursor, Aider) already ship multi-retriever architectures, validating the thesis by revealed preference. The cross-sector financial reasoning case — structurally identical to the user's +13-point cross-domain synthesis result — is **under-benchmarked in published literature**, making it the single strongest commercial white-space the analysis surfaces.

The necessary-conditions question resolves cleanly against the literature: **Adaptive-RAG, Self-RAG, and CRAG converge on a three-condition gate** (heterogeneous workload, ≥3 distinct cognitive tasks, measurable quality metric) that matches the user's hypothesis almost exactly. A 10-item builder's checklist with a 7/10 shipping threshold appears at the end.

## Legal AI: the most damning empirical case against monolithic RAG

The evidence against monolithic RAG in legal AI is overwhelming, and it is the only domain where a peer-reviewed empirical study of commercial production systems has been published at scale. Magesh, Surani, Dahl, Suzgun, Manning and Ho (Stanford RegLab, *Journal of Empirical Legal Studies* 2025, arXiv:2405.20362) preregistered over 200 queries against three flagship tools in May 2024 and found: **Lexis+ AI at 65% accuracy with ~17% hallucination and 18% incomplete; Westlaw AI-Assisted Research at 42% accuracy with ~33% hallucination (nearly double Lexis+); Ask Practical Law AI refusing or failing to answer >60% of queries**. An ungrounded GPT-4 baseline hallucinated at 43%. Hallucinations split between factually wrong statements and **misgrounding** — citing a real case that does not actually support the proposition offered — with authors attributing roughly 40–50% of hallucinations to retrieval-stage failure, not generation. Examples include Westlaw fabricating a Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure paragraph and Lexis+ applying *Casey*'s overruled undue-burden standard post-*Dobbs*. These are not prompt-tuning artifacts; they are **architectural failures of a single retrieval paradigm applied to cognitively heterogeneous tasks**.

The predecessor study, Dahl et al. "Large Legal Fictions" (*Journal of Legal Analysis* 2024, arXiv:2401.01301), exposes the task-type gradient directly. Pooled hallucination rates were 58% for GPT-4 and 88% for Llama 2, but the **per-task gradient is the key architectural signal**: low-complexity tasks (existence, court, citation, author lookups) had the lowest hallucination; moderate tasks (quotation, disposition, core legal question, overruling status) showed hallucination "surges"; high-complexity tasks (doctrinal agreement, procedural posture, subsequent history) performed near chance on binary questions. Models could not predict their own hallucinations — stated confidence was uncorrelated with accuracy — and they reinforced user-planted counterfactual premises.

The clearest architectural lesson comes from Afane et al. (CSLAW '26, arXiv:2603.03300) on **LaborBench**, a 50-state Department of Labor unemployment-insurance statutory-survey benchmark. **STARA**, a purpose-built structured-retrieval system, reached 83% Boolean accuracy (92% after correcting DOL omissions), while standard vanilla RAG hit 70%, **Westlaw AI scored 58%, and Lexis+ AI 64%**. Both commercial tools **underperformed generic RAG** on multi-jurisdictional surveys. Errors traced to retrieval failure plus reasoning confusion across related legal concepts. The Vals Legal AI Report (February 2025) corroborates heterogeneity from a different angle: Harvey Assistant scored 94.8% on Document Q&A and 80.2% on Chronology Generation — tying the lawyer baseline — but AI tools collectively lagged the lawyer baseline on EDGAR Research and Redlining. **Different tasks, different outcomes, identical model.**

LegalBench (Guha et al., NeurIPS 2023) formalized this gradient into the IRAC framework (Issue spotting, Rule recall, Rule application, Conclusion). The closed-vs-open-source gap is largest on rule-conclusion (multi-step reasoning) and smallest on rule-recall; GPT-4 beats GPT-3.5 and Claude-1 by 20+ absolute points on issue spotting (p<0.01). **These stages demand different treatments**: issue spotting is classification, authority lookup is structured retrieval, holding synthesis is graph traversal over citation networks, argument construction is generation, and cite-checking is deterministic verification.

The failure modes have moved from research curiosities to judicial sanctions. *Mata v. Avianca* (S.D.N.Y. 2023) sanctioned lawyers $5,000 under Rule 11 for filing six fabricated ChatGPT opinions. *Johnson v. Dunn* (N.D. Ala., July 2025) escalated to **disqualification of counsel** — a practice-group co-leader at a firm with an anti-AI policy — because monetary fines had proven ineffective. *U.S. v. Farris* (6th Cir. April 2026) sanctioned errors in a brief produced with Westlaw's CoCounsel, confirming that commercial RAG-grounded tools still produce sanctionable errors. Over 400 worldwide cases involving AI-fabricated citations have been tracked as of early 2026.

**Legal pipeline mapping (evidence strength in parentheses):**

| Stage | Cognitive task | Recommended architecture | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Issue spotting | Classification | Vector RAG over doctrinal corpus + classifier head | LegalBench issue-spotting results (strong) |
| Authority lookup / Shepardizing | Structured discovery | Citation-network graph retrieval or deterministic KG index | Dahl & Magesh on misgrounding (strong conceptual, thin empirical) |
| Statutory survey | Discovery + classification | Structured/statutory KG per jurisdiction | STARA 92% vs Westlaw 58% (strong) |
| Holding synthesis | Cross-document synthesis | Hybrid graph traversal + long-context + chain-of-verification | Dahl moderate-complexity surge (moderate) |
| Brief drafting | Generation | RAG + firm knowledge + rubric-guided generation | Vals VLAIR 77–95% (moderate) |
| Cite-check | Critique | Deterministic resolver (Eyecite) + CAG over authoritative corpus + reject-on-miss | Mata/Johnson sanctions + Magesh misgrounding data (strong) |

The unambiguous architectural lesson: **statutory retrieval needs structure, cite-checking needs determinism, argument construction needs generation, and applying one paradigm to all collapses quality by 20–30 points.** No peer-reviewed study yet directly compares citation-network graph retrieval against dense vector retrieval on U.S. case-law holdings under controlled conditions — a commercial white space.

## Finance: cross-sector synthesis is the richest white space

Financial AI shows the second-strongest monolithic-failure evidence and contains the user's most strategically important finding. FinanceBench (Islam et al. 2023, arXiv:2311.11944; Patronus AI/Contextual/Stanford, 10,231 questions across SEC filings) reports **81% incorrect-or-refused responses from GPT-4-Turbo with retrieval**, matching Llama 2's 81% — a near-total failure of monolithic RAG on single-firm financial QA. Prompt ordering alone swung GPT-4-Turbo accuracy from **25% to 78% by moving context before the question**, a red flag that context-window utilization is failing rather than reasoning. The benchmark explicitly scopes to single-firm single-turn, leaving cross-firm and cross-sector synthesis untested.

**HybridRAG** (Sarmah et al., BlackRock + NVIDIA, ICAIF 2024, arXiv:2408.04948) provides the cleanest head-to-head architecture comparison in finance. On Nifty 50 Q1 FY2024 earnings calls, VectorRAG and GraphRAG **win opposite question types on the same corpus**: GraphRAG hits 0.96 context precision on extractive questions where entities are explicit, but fails when no entity is mentioned; VectorRAG wins abstractive questions by 0.91 vs 0.89 answer relevance. The combined HybridRAG reaches 0.96 answer relevance — but context precision drops to 0.79 because naive concatenation injects noise. **The architecture that wins depends on the question; monolithic choices lose information by design.**

For cross-sector pattern detection — **structurally identical to the user's +13-point cross-domain synthesis win** — the most direct evidence comes from FinReflectKG-MultiHop (Arun et al. 2025, arXiv:2510.02906), which partitions S&P 100 10-K queries into intra-document (48.7%), inter-year same-company (41.6%), and cross-company same-year (9.7%). KG-guided precise retrieval beat page-window vector retrieval by **+24% correctness with 84.5% fewer tokens**. Counterintuitively, cross-company same-year was *easier* than inter-year same-company because GICS-aligned schemas make cross-entity metric comparison semantically clean, while inter-year comparisons fight evolving terminology. FCMR (arXiv:2412.12567) pushes the ceiling lower still: Claude 3.5 Sonnet reaches only **30.4% on Hard 3-hop cross-modal financial questions**, with the paper's authors identifying retrieval — not reasoning — as the dominant bottleneck. BRIDGE demonstrates the same pattern: ColPali retrieval *degraded* end-to-end multi-hop QA versus direct-context baseline.

The critical finding for the user's thesis: **no published benchmark isolates cross-sector contagion or cross-sector synthesis as a category**. FinReflectKG-MultiHop is the closest analog and only 9.7% of its items are cross-company; cross-sector specifically (banking capital ratios × energy reserves × semiconductor supply chains) is essentially unbenchmarked. The cross-sector financial network literature (DebtRank, supply-chain contagion graphs) exists but has not been integrated into LLM-RAG evaluations. **This is a publication-grade commercial white space**: the user's +13-point cross-domain synthesis gain is the structural analog of a capability finance actively needs and cannot currently benchmark.

**Finance pipeline mapping:**

| Stage | Cognitive task | Architecture | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| News monitoring | Freshness filtering | Dynamic temporal KG (FinDKG) + vector | Moderate (FinDKG, practitioner) |
| Single-filing extraction | 1-hop retrieval | VectorRAG + long-context, context-first | FinanceBench 25→78% swing (strong) |
| Numerical reasoning | Sequential calculation | Domain LLM + CoT + deterministic calculator | BloombergGPT, ConvFinQA (moderate) |
| Intra-company temporal | Cross-year alignment | Temporally-indexed KG (Inline XBRL) | FinReflectKG-MultiHop hardest bucket |
| **Cross-sector pattern detection** | **Cross-schema synthesis / long-range graph traversal** | **Agentic hybrid: dynamic KG + GNN + LLM synthesis** | **Thin — FCMR 30.4% ceiling; no direct benchmark** |
| Risk assessment | Structured calculation + reasoning | Graph (supply chain, ownership) + calculator tools | Moderate |
| Report generation | Synthesis + generation | Long-context LLM with reranked hybrid retrieval | Moderate |

## Medical: retriever × task interactions of 30 points

MedRAG (Xiong et al., ACL Findings 2024, arXiv:2402.13178) provides the cleanest per-retriever × per-task interaction data in any domain. Across 7,663 questions over MMLU-Med, MedQA-US, MedMCQA, PubMedQA, and BioASQ, four retrievers (BM25, Contriever, SPECTER, MedCPT) produce non-uniform wins. BM25 lifts PubMedQA from 36.0% (no retrieval) to **66.2% (+30.2 points)** and BioASQ from 74.3% to 88.5% (+14.2). SPECTER on the same PubMedQA hits only 54.8% — an **11.4-point retriever gap on the identical task**. MedCPT dominates the PubMed corpus overall. No single retriever wins every task; RRF fusion of BM25+MedCPT is the robust default. Textbooks and StatPearls help exam-style questions but hurt research QA. Beyond 32 retrieved snippets, the "lost-in-the-middle" effect degrades performance — and MedRAG sometimes *hurts* accuracy versus no retrieval (MMLU-Med: 72.91% → 72.27%).

The discovery-versus-lookup dichotomy appears starkly in drug interactions and contraindications. DrugBank encodes 365,984 drug–drug interaction triples across 86 relation types over 1,710+ drugs. These are relational triples by construction — link-prediction over a knowledge graph is the standard ML approach (KGNN, KnowDDI, DeepDDI). When LLMs retrieve over prose instead, failure modes compound. A pharmacovigilance study (PMC12084699, 82 positive + 22 negative ICSRs) found ChatGPT detected 99% of true DDIs and Claude/Gemini 95% — but **all LLMs failed the negative controls, inventing DDIs to explain adverse events**. A RAG-augmented GPT-4o-mini contraindication system (arXiv:2508.06145) scored **0.87 for pregnancy, 0.94 for age-related, and 0.89 for drug-drug interactions** — DDIs lowest because compositional reasoning is required while pregnancy contraindications are explicitly described. PlaceboBench (pharma SmPC RAG, 7 SOTA LLMs on EMA regulatory questions) found **hallucination rates of 26–64%** even with authoritative source documents in context.

Med-PaLM 2 (Singhal et al., *Nature Medicine* 2025) reached 86.5% on MedQA and 81.8% on PubMedQA with self-consistency — strong parametric performance, but label-noise limited further PubMedQA gains and the authors caution that benchmark performance does not translate to safe deployment. Nori et al. showed GPT-4 passes USMLE zero-shot but their breakdown is by USMLE content category, not by cognitive-task type, leaving the per-task retrieval question unaddressed in that paper.

**Medical pipeline mapping:** Symptom intake → dialogue LLM; differential diagnosis → BM25+MedCPT fusion over PubMed+textbooks (discovery over unstructured narrative); guideline recall → textbooks corpus + LLM; **contraindication and DDI checking → knowledge graph (DrugBank/RxNorm) with deterministic queries, not monolithic RAG**; dosing → deterministic calculator; patient communication → RAG grounded on patient leaflets. The DDI case is the sharpest: relational-completeness tasks fundamentally require relational retrieval, and the RAG-over-prose approach hallucinates interactions into existence in negative-control populations.

## Software engineering: production tools have already shipped the multi-architecture thesis

Software engineering offers the most interesting evidence pattern: **production tools have converged on multi-retriever architectures by revealed preference**, ahead of academic benchmarks. Sourcegraph Cody combines embeddings, SCIP-based code graphs, static analysis, BM25, and local editor context — and reports a 35% reduction in top-20 chunk retrieval failure via contextual embeddings. Cursor runs Merkle-tree incremental indexing with Tree-sitter syntactic chunking plus hybrid semantic + regex (ripgrep) at agent runtime. Aider uses **no embeddings at all** — only Tree-sitter AST with NetworkX PageRank over symbol-reference graphs, and achieves the highest context-utilization efficiency in third-party comparisons (4.3–6.5% vs Cursor's 14.7%). Cline runs a three-tier ripgrep + fzf + Tree-sitter stack. None of these use monolithic vector RAG.

The benchmarks support the revealed preference. CodeRAG-Bench (Wang et al., NAACL Findings 2025, arXiv:2406.14497) tested 10 retrievers across 10 LMs on 8 tasks and found **no single winner** — BM25 dominates repo-level lexical-overlap tasks, code-specific dense retrievers (CodeSage, voyage-code-2) win semantic code tasks, general text embedders win StackOverflow and tutorial retrieval. SWE-bench (Jimenez et al., ICLR 2024, arXiv:2310.06770) originally saw Claude 2 reach just 1.96% with BM25 and 4.8% with oracle retrieval — authors specifically identified that **"models are simply ineffective at localizing problematic code."** The current frontier (Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5) reaches 70–87% on SWE-bench Verified, but **SWE-Bench Pro crashes the same models to 23.3%**, and SWE-Bench+ with contamination cleaning drops SWE-Agent+GPT-4 from 12.47% to **0.55% on fully post-cutoff issues** — exposing how much of headline progress is solution leakage.

The bug-localization versus patch-generation split (SWE-Next trajectory classification: ~8.7% localization failures versus much larger patch-semantic buckets) confirms that different stages of the SWE pipeline fail for different reasons and benefit from different retrieval. cAST (arXiv:2506.15655) shows AST-aware chunking beats fixed-size chunking across retrievers. SpIDER (arXiv:2512.16956) shows graph-aware dense retrieval beats BM25 and vanilla dense across Python, Java, JavaScript, and TypeScript — specifically on graph-structured queries. Self-Route (arXiv:2407.16833) finds long-context beats RAG when resourced but hybrid routing achieves LC-quality at RAG-cost.

**SWE pipeline mapping:** Issue understanding → full-context LLM; **bug localization → AST/graph (Aider PageRank, RepoGraph, SpIDER) + BM25 lexical** — dense-only insufficient for multi-hop dependency tracing; cross-file context gathering → multi-retriever (Cody model); patch generation → long-context LLM with oracle-like retrieval plus reflection; test/verification → shell-executing agents, not retrieval-bound. **The fact that every leading production tool has independently converged on multi-retriever is itself strong evidence for the user's thesis** — stronger than any single benchmark paper.

## Enterprise knowledge management: the least academically-grounded case, but Klarna is a natural experiment

Enterprise KM has the thinnest academic evidence base but directionally consistent industry data. MIT NANDA's "GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025" (Challapally et al., July 2025) reports **95% of enterprise GenAI pilots deliver zero measurable P&L impact**, with vendor solutions reaching production ~67% of the time versus ~33% for internal builds. Methodology critiques (Futuriom, Wharton's Kevin Werbach) argue the 95% headline exceeds what the data supports — treat it as directional. Gartner (July 2024) predicts ≥30% of GenAI projects will be abandoned post-POC by end-2025, and (June 2025) **over 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end-2027**, citing "agent washing." A November 2025 Gartner I&O survey (n=782) found only 28% of AI use cases fully succeed and 20% fail outright; BCG (September 2025) reports 74% of enterprises have yet to show tangible value.

**Neither Glean nor Microsoft 365 Copilot is framing enterprise KM as a multi-architecture problem by persona.** Glean publishes a hybrid single pipeline (BM25 + learned semantic vector + enterprise knowledge graph with PageRank-inspired authority signals) with personalization as a reranking layer, not an architecture swap. Microsoft Copilot runs a two-pass Sydney orchestrator over a unified Semantic Index with lexical + semantic + hybrid retrieval; persona differentiation happens via permissions (Sensitivity Labels, DLP) and connectors, not retrieval-paradigm swaps. The academic persona-aware-retrieval literature (PersonaRAG, arXiv:2407.09394; ID-RAG, arXiv:2509.25299) exists but is thin — industry preprints, simulation-based validation, no enterprise-scale head-to-head.

The Klarna reversal is the cleanest natural experiment. February 2024: Klarna's OpenAI-powered chatbot handled 2.3M conversations in its first month — "the work of 700 agents" — enabling headcount reduction from 5,500 to 3,400. May 2025: CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski reverses publicly to Bloomberg: **"lower quality"**; hiring restarts. September 2025: launch of an "Uber-style" remote human agent pool for escalation. The architectural interpretation matches the user's persona hypothesis precisely: tier-1 FAQ volume is CAG-appropriate (stable, small, cache-friendly), but complex or emotional escalations require different retrieval and reasoning — a single architecture collapsed on the latter. *Moffatt v. Air Canada* (2024 BCCRT 149) establishes legal liability for chatbot misrepresentation — a chatbot cannot be treated as a "separate legal entity" — raising the cost of monolithic deployment quality failures. McDonald's IBM drive-through hit ~85% order accuracy and was shut down in June 2024.

"Lost in the Middle" (Liu et al., TACL 2024) directly implies that compliance and full-context workflows still require reranking because relevant information degrades in middle positions even for 100K-context models. This is the evidence for the compliance-persona arm of the hypothesis.

**Enterprise persona mapping (mostly directional/practitioner-grounded):** Support agents → CAG over stable FAQ/policy (Chan et al. 2024, arXiv:2412.15605); report writers → graph/KG-RAG for cross-entity synthesis; **compliance/audit → full-context with reranker to avoid lost-in-middle**; executives → summary digest over retrieval. The user's framing is **genuinely novel**: no major analyst or vendor is publicly articulating persona-to-architecture mapping as the design principle, making this the most commercially differentiated position of the five domains, and the one with the least academic backing.

## Necessary conditions: academic framing and builder's checklist

### Academic framing

Adaptive-RAG (Jeong et al., NAACL 2024, arXiv:2403.14403) routes across no-retrieval, single-step, and multi-step paths via a small T5 classifier. Its classifier is noisy — diagonal accuracies of 66%/65%/31% — yet still net-positive because mis-routing to a more expensive path is costly but not catastrophic. Oracle-classifier upper-bounds demonstrate headroom: routing is classifier-limited, not architecture-limited. **The gains require a mixed-complexity workload**; on uniformly easy or uniformly hard traffic, a single strategy is Pareto-optimal.

Self-RAG (Asai et al., ICLR 2024, arXiv:2310.11511) ablations show retrieval necessity is task-dependent: disabling retrieval caused a ~40% relative accuracy drop on PopQA (long-tail open-domain) but only ~2% on PubHealth (fact-verification where parametric knowledge suffices). Indiscriminate top-1 retrieval underperformed adaptive retrieval on mixed workloads. CRAG (Yan et al., arXiv:2401.15884) reproduction studies show the **Correct-action subset hits 78.1% vs 51.4% vanilla RAG (+26.7 points)** — but Religion questions sit at 5.0% even when the evaluator is confident, documenting a canonical **router-failure mode** where classifier confidence is a poor proxy for answer correctness.

Mallen et al. ("When Not to Trust LMs," ACL 2023, PopQA) established the popularity-thresholded adaptive-retrieval approach: +5.3 points over the best non-adaptive method, +10 points over always-retrieve, with **up to 50% API cost reduction**. Crucially, adaptive gains are **increasing in model size** — larger LMs memorize popular entities and benefit more from knowing when to skip retrieval. GraphRAG-Bench (arXiv:2506.05690) shows no paradigm universally wins: GraphRAG hits 90.2% on MuSiQue factual versus NaiveRAG's 11.1%, but reverses to 83.7% vs 70.7% on QuALITY. Han et al. (2025) report GraphRAG is **13.4 points worse than vanilla RAG on Natural Questions** with 2.3× latency — demonstrating that routing to the wrong architecture is actively harmful. RAGRouter-Bench (arXiv:2602.00296) achieves 0.928 macro-F1 paradigm routing with 28.1% token savings.

The MoE analog is instructive: DeepSeek-V3's auxiliary-loss-free balancing (arXiv:2408.15664) addresses **routing collapse** — self-reinforcing expert imbalance where one path monopolizes traffic and others degrade silently. Shared-expert patterns (always-on fallback) transfer directly: any production routing RAG should keep a vanilla-RAG default path.

**This literature validates the user's three-condition hypothesis almost verbatim:** heterogeneous workload (Adaptive-RAG, Mallen), ≥3 cognitive stages (Adaptive-RAG's 3 paths, CRAG's 3 actions, Self-RAG's retrieve/critique/generate), and a measurable quality metric (every successful routing paper depends on one to set thresholds).

### Builder's checklist

Use the following ten items. Each is empirically grounded; the scoring heuristic follows.

**1. Workload heterogeneity.** Your traffic must span a measurable mix of query types (parametric-answerable, single-hop, multi-hop/compositional). Adaptive-RAG's gains vanish on homogeneous workloads. Profile actual query distribution before investing in routing. *Weight: 2 points.*

**2. At least three cognitive stages with distinct failure modes.** Adaptive-RAG uses three strategies, CRAG three actions, Self-RAG three operations. Below three stages, a single well-tuned advanced-RAG pipeline usually dominates and routing overhead is unamortizable. Beyond roughly five stages, cascading failures from the Layer6 error taxonomy (16 error types across 4 stages) dominate gains. *Weight: 2 points.*

**3. Query-type distribution heterogeneity.** If any single bucket (factual, reasoning, summary, creative) exceeds ~70% of volume, tune the dominant architecture and skip routing. Routing pays when no single paradigm wins ≥75% of query types. *Weight: 1 point.*

**4. Knowledge-base structure too rich for a single access pattern.** Heuristic: if your KB has (i) entity graph >10k nodes, (ii) dense relational structure, and (iii) queries requiring ≥2 hops, add a graph path alongside vector retrieval. Otherwise dual-level retrieval (LightRAG-style) suffices. Structure alone is insufficient — structure plus query-type variance is what justifies multi-architecture. *Weight: 2 points.*

**5. Measurable quality metric.** The single hardest and most predictive gate. Without EM/F1, FactScore, calibrated LLM-as-judge (with position-bias controls), or domain-grounded accuracy, you cannot set routing thresholds, detect drift, or calculate the oracle upper-bound that proves routing has headroom. The user's calibrated LLM-as-judge with Spearman ρ=0.841 against a 5-model editorial panel is exactly this gate. **No metric, no routing.** *Weight: 2 points.*

**6. Router accuracy floor.** The router must beat (a) random = 1/K and (b) majority-class baseline weighted by cost-of-error. For three-way routing, target ≥0.9 macro-F1 (TF-IDF+SVM hit 0.928 in RAGRouter-Bench). Adaptive-RAG shipped net-positive at 31% class-level accuracy on its hardest class, so this floor depends on the relative cost of mis-routing. *Weight: 1 point.*

**7. Cost tolerance exceeds overhead.** Only route when expected savings from skipping expensive paths > (router compute × query volume) + (mis-route penalty × error rate). Mallen's 50% API cost reduction on PopQA is a usable upper-bound benchmark. GraphRAG indexing can run into tens of thousands of dollars on large corpora — amortization requires sustained traffic. *Weight: 1 point.*

**8. Latency budget permits the slow path.** GraphRAG adds ~2.3× latency versus NaiveRAG. If P95 < 1s is required (call-center, real-time chat), routing to a slow architecture even 20% of the time blows the SLO. Define per-path latency budgets and use CRAG-style confidence thresholds to short-circuit. *Weight: 1 point.*

**9. Failure-mode instrumentation before shipping.** Track per-path query share to detect router collapse (one path exceeding 90% of traffic signals the router should be killed). Monitor retrieval thrash (>3 iterations without score improvement), tool storms (>N tool calls per query — documented cases of $50–$200 burned in 10 minutes with 200 LLM calls), context bloat (>60% of context filled with retrieved tokens). Ship a shared-expert fallback — a vanilla-RAG path always available. Implement span-level citation validation; silent attribution errors are the most-missed failure mode. *Weight: 1 point.*

**10. Distribution-shift monitoring.** Classifiers trained on silver labels drift with query distribution. Require offline recalibration when per-path acceptance rate shifts >15% week-over-week or when a held-out canary set drops >2 points EM. *Weight: 1 point.*

**Ship routing if score ≥ 7/10.** Items 1, 2, 4, 5 are gating criteria worth 2 points each; items 3, 6, 7, 8 are tuning criteria worth 1 point; items 9 and 10 are operational prerequisites worth 1 point. The user's validated system scores confidently against this — analytical essay generation clearly has heterogeneous workload, ≥3 distinct cognitive stages (classification, discovery, synthesis, generation, critique — exactly matching Adaptive-RAG's dimensional structure but richer), a structured behavioral-science KB, and a calibrated quality metric with ρ=0.841 against expert panel. The +2.1 mean and +13 cross-domain synthesis gains are consistent with Mallen's +10 always-retrieve delta and FinReflectKG's +24% KG-guided retrieval advantage, at the high end rather than the ceiling.

## Conclusion: generalization is supported, legal is the proof, cross-sector is the white space

The evidence is not ambiguous. Monolithic RAG fails in legal AI at rates of 17–33% on flagship commercial tools; in financial QA at 81% on GPT-4-Turbo; at 26–64% on pharmaceutical regulatory questions; at 57.6–80% resolution on SWE-bench Verified that collapses to 23% on Pro and 0.55% on contamination-cleaned post-cutoff issues. Every domain examined shows **retriever × task interactions large enough to dominate model choice**: 30 points in MedRAG, 24% in FinReflectKG, 20+ points in LegalBench issue spotting versus open-source models, and entire reversals in GraphRAG-Bench. Production software-engineering tools have already shipped multi-retriever architectures by revealed preference, confirming the thesis before academia caught up. The user's necessary conditions — three stages, structured KB, measurable metric — map almost one-to-one onto Adaptive-RAG, Self-RAG, and CRAG's operational findings.

The single most important strategic insight is negative: **cross-sector pattern detection in finance is essentially unbenchmarked in published literature**, despite being structurally identical to the user's +13-point cross-domain synthesis result. FCMR caps at 30.4% on hard 3-hop cross-modal questions; FinReflectKG-MultiHop contains under 10% cross-company items; DebtRank-style contagion networks have not been integrated into LLM-RAG evaluations. A paper that benchmarks cross-sector synthesis with a per-stage architecture selector against monolithic BloombergGPT, HybridRAG, and commercial financial research tools would occupy a publication-grade gap. The enterprise persona-to-architecture mapping is the other untaken position — neither Glean nor Copilot is articulating it, and MIT NANDA's disputed-but-directional 95% failure headline plus Klarna's public reversal make the commercial case without academic cover. Legal is where the evidence is overwhelming; finance and enterprise are where the capability is not yet on the shelf.
