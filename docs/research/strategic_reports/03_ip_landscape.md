# File the provisional, publish second, Apache the rest

**The right move is a $65 pro-se US provisional filed this week, an arXiv post 2–4 weeks later, an Apache 2.0 code release after that, a trademark on a brandable name, and trade-secret discipline around the judge calibration corpus.** This sequence costs under $2,500 in year one, preserves every monetization path (consulting, OSS+services, SaaS), and buys a 12-month window to decide whether to spend $10–15k on a non-provisional. Against that, pure defensive publication is cheaper but forecloses foreign patent rights permanently once you post, and a serious patent-first strategy is unjustifiable for a solo founder because enforcement of a software method patent starts at ~$300k and detection of per-stage routing inside a competitor's SaaS is near-impossible. The patent question at your scale is not *can I enforce this* — it's *is a $65 option worth holding*. It is, but only if you treat it as optionality, not as a moat.

The underlying landscape supports this. No US patent currently reads on the specific four-way combination — per-stage routing, cognitive-task-type classification, heterogeneous knowledge-access architectures including a full-context cache, and a calibrated LLM-judge arbitrating selection. But the adjacent academic art (AutoRAG, DSPy, Self-RAG, CRAG, Adaptive-RAG, Modular RAG survey) is thick enough that a broad independent claim will not survive §103. Your peers — LangChain, LlamaIndex, Weaviate, Qdrant, Chroma — have filed **zero** patents between them at seed and early stages. That tells you what the market has already decided: in AI infra at your stage, brand, community, and the closed commercial layer are the real moats. The provisional is a hedge, not a strategy.

## The patent landscape has room, but narrow room

US searches across Google Patents, USPTO PPUBS, and incidental WIPO surface **one real obstacle and a half-dozen secondary ones**. The real obstacle is Microsoft's GraphRAG family — **US20250131289A1 and WO2025085237A1**, priority October 2023 — which claims local/community/global query-mode selection over a combined KG+vector index. It is distinguishable because it routes *per query*, covers only two heterogeneous stores, and does not invoke a cognitive-task taxonomy or a calibrated judge. Secondary threats include **US12111859B2** (enterprise generative AI orchestrator, plan-driven routing by data modality), **US20240346256A1** (Microsoft baseline RAG), and pre-LLM query-routing art (US8620938B2). Crucially, **no US patent was found on calibrated LLM-as-judge as a runtime selector** — big tech has chosen trade-secret and open-source routes here. That's a genuine white-space signal, though it also means examiners will lean hard on academic NPL when they find it.

Territory staking is concentrated in Microsoft. Google, Meta, Nvidia, OpenAI, Anthropic, and Databricks are **patent-light** in this niche; Amazon and Salesforce are product-forward but absent from the filings surfaced. That asymmetry matters for your defensive analysis: you are not walking into a thicket, you are walking into mostly empty ground with one adjacent filer.

## The academic prior art is the real §103 problem

The honest read is that **a broad independent claim on "per-stage architecture selection driven by cognitive task type via calibrated judge" will not issue**. The blocking combination is predictable under KSR: AutoRAG (Kim et al., Oct 2024) already teaches per-stage module selection driven by metrics, though offline and dataset-level rather than runtime; DSPy (Khattab et al., ICLR 2024) composes architecturally different modules per stage with judge-as-metric optimizers; CRAG and Self-RAG both use judge-like signals to gate pipeline actions; Adaptive-RAG routes by task classification; and the Modular RAG survey (Gao et al., 2024) explicitly theorizes routing and scheduling of heterogeneous modules across pipeline stages. A skilled practitioner holding AutoRAG + Adaptive-RAG + CRAG would reach your combination with reasonable expectation of success. **The five-way cognitive taxonomy (classification / discovery / synthesis / generation / critique) is not itself patentably distinctive** — it maps onto Bloom-adjacent categories that have existed in IR/NLP for decades.

What remains defensible is narrow. A claim reciting a **specific enumerated mapping** from each cognitive task type to a named architectural family, plus a **specific calibration procedure** for the judge (not merely the ρ=0.841 number, which is an outcome not a mechanism), plus **runtime** per-stage dispatch that distinguishes from AutoRAG's offline search and DSPy's compile-time optimization, plus the inclusion of **full-context cache as a peer** to vector/graph/wiki-index, is the claim surface that could survive both §102 anticipation and §103 obviousness. This is a narrow patent. It is not a general platform claim.

## §101 survivability is moderate and draftable

**Recentive Analytics v. Fox Corp.** (Fed. Cir. April 2025, cert. denied Dec 2025) is the controlling shadow. It killed "applying ML to a new data environment" claims and specifically rejected iterative training and dynamic updates as conferring eligibility. A naive framing of your invention — "select an AI retrieval method based on task classification" — reads as exactly that abstract idea and dies. But the invention has three elements that can be framed as a concrete technical improvement under the *Enfish/McRO* line: specific heterogeneous architectures with quantifiably different operational profiles, a calibrated judge with a measured correlation threshold producing a control signal, and **measured performance delta (+2.1 mean, +13 on cross-domain synthesis)** that directly rebuts the "no technical improvement" attack. USPTO examination under the December 2025 Squires memo and updated MPEP §101 guidance is notably more applicant-friendly than Federal Circuit litigation, so the realistic failure mode is not prosecution — it's validity if you ever try to enforce.

Enforcement is where the patent strategy collapses for a solo founder. **Per the AIPLA 2023 Economic Survey, a sub-$1M patent suit costs ~$300k through claim construction and ~$600k through trial; software patents run to the high end.** Contingency firms cherry-pick, and a solo founder with a single narrow software patent is not an attractive book. The deeper problem is detection: per-stage architecture selection happens server-side inside a SaaS black box. You cannot reverse-engineer from API outputs whether a competitor classifies stages by cognitive task type or what judge they use. Design-around is trivial — rename the taxonomy, substitute a fifth architecture, and you've evaded. **Budget as if you will never enforce this patent.** Its realistic value is as a fundraising asset, an M&A signaling tool, and a defensive deterrent.

## Provisional economics are where the math works

A pro-se US provisional at micro-entity rates is **$65 in USPTO fees** (not $300 — micro-entity is the correct bucket for a pre-revenue solo founder under the $251,190 gross income threshold, with ≤4 prior applications, confirmed via Form SB/15A). That's a less-than-a-day-of-burn cost for a 12-month priority option. Drafting quality is the real input: expect **40–60 hours of founder time** to produce a provisional that survives *Ariad*-level written-description scrutiny. The single most common pro-se failure is filing a slide deck or whitepaper as the spec — technically legal, regularly fatal to priority. The provisional must disclose multiple embodiments (at least three task-type taxonomies, at least four architectures with mechanical detail including HNSW/IVF index types for vector and typed-edge semantics for graph, at least three judge implementations with calibration procedures, pseudocode for the selection policy, and the +13 performance data with methodology).

The 12-month decision point is the actual strategic moment. **Non-provisional conversion with competent software/AI-patent counsel runs $10–15k end-to-end for drafting and filing** (Schell IP, Triangle IP, and PowerPatent quotes converge on this range in 2025–2026; BigLaw is $15–25k+), plus $3–8k in office-action responses over 2–3 years of prosecution. Government fees at micro-entity are roughly $364 for filing+search+examination. A PCT filing to preserve international optionality adds ~$1,900 in WIPO/USPTO fees plus $1,200–$3,000 in attorney prep; national-phase entry at month 30 is where PCT costs explode to $5–10k+ per country. **Do not attempt the "second provisional to extend" trick** — Paris Convention Article 4C anchors priority to the first filing, and any chained provisionals break the chain while intervening disclosures become prior art.

One subtle but critical foreclosure point: provisionals themselves are **never published** by the USPTO. If you abandon at month 12, there is zero public disclosure from the filing. This means the provisional does not interfere with a trade-secret path, and it does not itself create defensive prior art — for that you need to publish (arXiv, IP.com, or Research Disclosure). The arXiv post is what creates the prior-art blockade against competitors; the provisional is what preserves your own ability to patent in spite of that post.

## Publication timing is the single decision that cannot be undone

Under AIA §102(b)(1)(A) your own disclosure within 12 months of your US filing is excepted as prior art against you — **in the US only**. Most foreign jurisdictions (EPO, China, Japan, Korea) have **absolute novelty with no grace period**. A pre-filing arXiv post permanently destroys your foreign patent rights. Same-day provisional-then-arXiv is safe; arXiv-then-provisional is fatal abroad even if the US remains theoretically salvageable. This is the single asymmetric risk that justifies filing the provisional first even if you are 90% sure you will never convert. The cost of getting this sequence wrong is six-figure international patent exposure; the cost of getting it right is $65 and a week of drafting.

## Apache 2.0 dominates the license analysis

Of the four real license choices, Apache 2.0 is the unambiguous winner for a solo founder with undecided monetization. **Section 3 grants users an express, royalty-free, irrevocable patent license scoped narrowly to patent claims necessarily infringed by the specific contribution** — it does not auto-license your other patents or future improvements you write separately. The defensive termination clause automatically revokes that license for any user who sues the project for patent infringement, giving you a free patent shield without any affirmative enforcement cost. Critically, if you later patent improvements that were not part of the Apache-released codebase, you retain full patent rights against anyone using those improvements. This is precisely the "retain optionality" behavior your situation demands.

MIT lacks the explicit patent grant and leaves the implicit-license question judicially unresolved. AGPL v3 is correct if your **only** path is SaaS with a network-copyleft moat against hyperscaler cloning, but Google's internal AGPL ban and pervasive enterprise procurement resistance will kill the consulting path and most enterprise OSS+services motion. BSL/SSPL/Elastic-style source-available licenses work only from a position of established adoption — **HashiCorp's August 2023 BSL switch triggered the OpenTofu fork within weeks**, MongoDB's 2018 SSPL move cost Debian and Red Hat distribution, Elastic's 2021 Apache→SSPL shift produced OpenSearch and they reluctantly re-added AGPLv3 in 2024 to rebuild OSS credibility. **Starting a brand-new project under BSL is a strategic error** — you have no network effects to protect and no trust buffer to spend. Permissive at launch, closed adjacent service for monetization, and a defensive patent option held privately is the peer-validated pattern.

## Peer companies have already solved this

The evidence across LangChain (MIT, zero confirmed patents, aggressive trademark portfolio of 5+ USPTO applications including LANGSMITH Ser. 98141268, closed LangSmith as commercial adjacent), LlamaIndex (MIT, zero confirmed patents, closed LlamaParse), Weaviate (BSD-3 since 2016, zero patents, closed cloud service), Qdrant (Apache 2.0, zero patents, closed cloud), and Chroma (Apache 2.0, zero patents, closed cloud) is consistent to the point of being mechanical. **The AI-infra-at-seed pattern is permissive OSS + trademark + closed commercial adjacent + no patents.** Pinecone is the single exception — closed-source from day one, no OSS core — and Edo Liberty's 75+ prior patents are all assigned to Yahoo and AWS, not Pinecone Systems. No founder in this cohort has publicly expressed regret about declining to patent; no peer company has been sued for patent infringement on their core routing or retrieval mechanics. Jeff Huber of Chroma stated the position most explicitly: *"It shouldn't be fake open source... we went with Apache 2. Open-core with closed control plane is a short-term optimization."*

The license-switch histories also carry a clean warning. Every late switch from permissive to restrictive produced a fork when the project had independent commercial users. Solo founders with monetization uncertainty should **not pre-commit to a restrictive license to guard against hypothetical AWS cloning** — if you're successful enough for AWS to care, you'll have other options; if you're not, the restrictive license just suppressed adoption.

## Trade secret the calibration corpus, not the framework

The judge's ρ=0.841 number is already publishable — once in an arXiv paper it has no economic value as a secret. **The defensible trade secret is the labeled corpus and methodology that produced that correlation**, plus any fine-tuned judge weights, operational threshold values tuned per domain, and the specific judge prompts. These stay in a private repo with 2FA and encryption at rest; they never enter the public Apache 2.0 codebase; they are covered by NDAs with any contractor or client. Critical legal detail most founders miss: **include the DTSA §1833(b) whistleblower notice in every NDA and contractor agreement** — its absence forfeits exemplary damages and attorneys' fees under the Defend Trade Secrets Act, and adding it costs nothing. The *Samuel Sherbrooke v. Mayer* decision (4th Cir., November 2025) confirms that confidentiality clauses alone can plausibly plead "reasonable measures" at the pleading stage, which is exactly the bar a solo founder needs to clear.

This stance survives all three monetization paths. Consulting clients never need access to the corpus. An Apache 2.0 release can ship the framework interfaces and an example untuned judge for tutorials while the production calibration stays server-side. A SaaS build keeps the entire judge pipeline closed. The only failure mode is accidental disclosure — a single arXiv appendix, a careless GitHub commit, a demo video that reveals threshold values — and the remedy is disciplined pre-release audit.

## Trademark the brand, not the technique

Do not file on "Cognitive Architecture Selection." **It's merely descriptive under §2(e)(1) and will draw an immediate USPTO refusal**, and placement on the Supplemental Register confers no federal presumptions worth the $350 filing fee. File on a suggestive or arbitrary coined brand name — the *Abercrombie* spectrum rewards distinctiveness strongly, and TEAS Plus/Standard were eliminated effective January 2025 with a single $350 per-class base fee. Class 9 (downloadable software) plus Class 42 (SaaS and consulting services) is the right dual filing if you want coverage across all three monetization paths, at $700 total. File intent-to-use (§1(b)) if you have not yet shipped commercially; §1(a) use-based if you have. Budget approximately $500 pro-se via the USPTO Trademark Center or $1,000–$2,000 with a flat-fee trademark boutique. Clear the mark on TESS, Google, GitHub, and domain registrars before filing to avoid the $125-per-extension treadmill when the SOU comes due.

## The 12-month action plan

The concrete 12-month sequence below is the synthesis of everything above. The first two steps are non-negotiable and time-sensitive; the others sequence around them.

At month 11, the branching decision resolves on observed traction. If consulting alone is working, let the provisional lapse — the arXiv post has already neutralized the patent space against competitors, and you save $10–15k. If OSS has meaningful adoption (low thousands of GitHub stars, active contributors), let the provisional lapse and double down on the Apache 2.0 core plus a managed-service commercial adjacent; do not switch licenses. If concrete SaaS pilots with paying customers have materialized, **convert the provisional to a narrow non-provisional at $10–15k attorney cost**, claiming the specific 4-tuple (≥4 heterogeneous stores including full-context cache × per-stage runtime decision × enumerated cognitive-task taxonomy × calibrated judge with specific calibration procedure), and cite Microsoft US20250131289A1, Adaptive-RAG, AutoRAG, DSPy, and CRAG in your IDS to pre-empt the obviousness attack. File a PCT only if there is a concrete international customer or partner story; otherwise the $5–10k per country national-phase exposure dwarfs the option value.

## What this strategy gets you and what it costs

The total year-one out-of-pocket under this plan is **approximately $1,500–$2,500**, the deferred year-two decision is between $0 (abandon) and $10–15k (convert), and the preserved optionality covers all three monetization paths without foreclosing any. The provisional is a $65 option on a $10–15k decision with real downside insurance against a competitor stake twelve months out. The arXiv post is the defensive publication that neutralizes the patent space against everyone else. The Apache 2.0 release with retained trade secrets on the calibration corpus replicates the peer-validated LangChain/Chroma/Weaviate/Qdrant/LlamaIndex model. The trademark on a coined brand name is the only IP register that pairs cleanly with all other strategies and all three paths. Trade secret discipline on the judge corpus creates the only real technical moat you can defend with a solo-founder budget.

The strategy to reject is not "file a patent" — the $65 option is trivially worth taking. The strategies to reject are (a) filing a non-provisional now without validated monetization, which spends $10–15k on a patent you cannot realistically enforce and that your peers have uniformly declined to file; (b) publishing without a prior provisional, which destroys foreign patent rights permanently for $0 of benefit; (c) launching under BSL or AGPL out of hypothetical hyperscaler fear, which suppresses adoption and breaks the consulting path; and (d) trademarking the descriptive term, which burns $350 on a filing that will be refused.

The core insight is that the decision the user framed as "patent vs defensive publication" is a false binary. **Provisional plus publication is both**, for $65 and a week of drafting time, and that is what the evidence says to do.
