You are extracting structured psychological research findings from a research
document for a knowledge base organized around human anxiety.

OUTPUT: A JSON array of finding objects. Output ONLY the JSON array — no
markdown, no preamble, no explanation, no trailing commentary. The array must
be directly parseable by json.loads().

============================================================
EXTRACTION RULES
============================================================

- Extract only discrete, empirically-grounded claims with cited sources
- Each finding must be a single atomic assertion — one claim, one mechanism,
  one piece of evidence
- Skip narrative transitions, section headers, and methodology descriptions
- Skip claims without at least one citable source in the document
- Extract every discrete empirical claim the document actually supports with
  citation — no more, no less. Do not pad with weak claims to hit a target
  count. The right number of findings depends entirely on the document's
  content: a focused 3,000-word document may yield 5-10 findings, a dense
  review article may yield 25+, and an opinion piece with no empirical
  grounding may yield 0. If the document does not support a finding with
  a citation, that finding does not belong in the output.

============================================================
INPUT FORMAT EXPECTATIONS
============================================================

You will typically receive a long-form research document with:
- Numbered or titled sections walking through distinct findings
- Inline citations in formats like (Author, Year) or (Smith et al., 2019,
  JPSP 117:3) or footnote-style references
- Narrative mechanism explanations interleaved with empirical claims
- Hedging language ("research suggests", "evidence indicates") — convert
  these to confidence_score values, not into the claim text
- Embedded confidence assessments ("robust evidence", "replicated across
  multiple samples", "preliminary finding") — use these as input to
  confidence_score and confidence_basis

Your job is to read through the document and produce one finding object per
empirically-supported atomic claim. A single paragraph may contain zero, one,
or multiple findings depending on how many distinct empirical assertions it
makes.

============================================================
REQUIRED FIELDS FOR EVERY FINDING
============================================================

claim (string, max 280 characters)
----------------------------------
One atomic empirical assertion. Present tense, declarative. NO hedging
language ("may", "could", "suggests", "appears to", "is thought to"). If the
original source hedges, reflect that in confidence_score instead of in the
claim.

BAD:  "Research suggests that loss aversion might play a role in decision-making"
GOOD: "Losses are weighted ~2x gains (λ≈1.955 across 607 estimates); a
       meta-analysis confirms the roughly 2:1 ratio though individual
       re-analyses find λ closer to 1.3"

BAD:  "Studies indicate exposure to mortality reminders can influence political
       attitudes"
GOOD: "Mortality salience produces a large effect (r=.50 across 31 experiments)
       on political attitudes, increasing support for charismatic leaders by
       up to 800% and strengthening worldview defense"

Include specific numbers, effect sizes, and sample sizes in the claim when the
document provides them. Precision is a feature.

elaboration (string)
--------------------
The mechanism — why this happens, what the underlying process is. NOT a
restatement of the claim. Should explain the psychological or biological
machinery the finding depends on, including:
- What causes what
- Which brain regions, neurotransmitters, or cognitive processes are involved
- Boundary conditions or moderators mentioned in the document
- Known contradicting evidence or replication concerns

confidence_score (float, 0.45-0.95)
-----------------------------------
0.85-0.95: Multiple independent replications, large samples, strong causal
           evidence, meta-analytic consensus
0.70-0.84: Single high-quality study, or multiple lower-quality studies
           converging, or well-established theoretical framework with
           empirical support
0.55-0.69: Preliminary evidence, small samples, significant replication
           concerns, or single-study findings without replication
0.45-0.54: Theoretically plausible but limited empirical support, or
           well-known effects with serious replication challenges

NEVER output a score below 0.45 — exclude the finding entirely instead.
NEVER output a score above 0.95 — 0.95 is the ceiling for honest social
science confidence.

confidence_basis (string)
-------------------------
REQUIRED FORMAT: three clause-separated components, in this order:
1. Study type (RCT / meta-analysis / observational cohort / fMRI / cross-cultural
   survey / etc.)
2. Sample or scope (N=847 / 277 experiments / 37 cultures / three independent
   labs / etc.)
3. Replication status or known concerns (replicated twice / failed Many Labs
   4 at ~43% FDR / original study only / mixed replication / etc.)

BAD:  "well-supported by research"
BAD:  "Peer-reviewed with two studies"
GOOD: "Meta-analysis, 210 studies / 186,073 respondents, strong cross-cultural
       replication, consistent effect sizes across samples"
GOOD: "RCT, N=1,550 across 17 labs (Many Labs 4), failed to replicate;
       original effect now estimated at ~43% FDR"
GOOD: "Cross-species ESB mapping, multiple species including cat/monkey/human
       case reports, coherent neurochemistry replicated across labs"

If the document does not provide enough information for all three components,
supply what you have and note the gap in the third slot
("original study only, replication status unknown").

root_anxieties (array, 1-2 values)
----------------------------------
MUST be from this exact list only:
  mortality, isolation, insignificance, meaninglessness, helplessness

Choose the anxiety this finding most directly relates to. If it clearly spans
two, include both. Never more than two.

primary_circuits (array, 0-3 values)
------------------------------------
MUST be from this exact list only:
  SEEKING, RAGE, FEAR, LUST, CARE, PANIC_GRIEF, PLAY

Based on Panksepp's affective neuroscience. Use empty array [] if no circuit
is clearly implicated by the finding. Do not speculate. A finding about
buyer decision-making probably does not need a circuit tag. A finding about
separation distress clearly needs PANIC_GRIEF.

academic_discipline (string)
----------------------------
MUST be from this list of 15 disciplines currently in the knowledge base:
  affective neuroscience
  b2b psychology
  behavioral economics
  buyer psychology
  clinical psychology
  cognitive psychology
  entrepreneurship psychology
  evolutionary psychology
  existential psychology
  health psychology
  moral psychology
  organizational psychology
  political psychology
  social neuroscience
  social psychology

If the finding clearly comes from a discipline NOT in this list (e.g.,
neuroeconomics, developmental psychology, decision science), use the closest
match from the list above AND add a note at the end of confidence_basis
in the format: " [note: finding originates from {actual_discipline}]"

cultural_domains (array)
------------------------
MUST use ONLY values from this exact list — no variations, no additions,
no plural/singular variants, no synonyms:

  religion
  legacy arts
  heroism
  immortality technology
  medicine
  tribalism
  nationalism
  romantic love
  social media
  friendship
  achievement culture
  wealth
  fame
  competitive systems
  legacy
  philosophy
  science
  ideology
  conspiracy theories
  narrative art
  political movements
  rebellion
  technology
  authoritarianism
  addiction

Tag the finding with 1-4 of these cultural domains — the real-world spheres
where this finding's dynamics visibly manifest. If the finding is a pure lab
mechanism with no clear cultural manifestation, use [].

EXAMPLES:
- Loss aversion finding → ["wealth", "competitive systems"]
- Mortality salience finding → ["religion", "political movements"]
- Social exclusion neural response → ["social media", "friendship"]
- Rat play circuit anatomy → []  (pure lab, no cultural sphere)

era (string)
------------
The decade or period when the primary research was conducted. Examples:
"1970s", "1990s", "2000s-2010s", "2020s", "post-WWII", "1960s-1970s".

INFER from publication years in sources if not explicitly stated. If a
source is dated 2009, the era is "2000s". If findings span multiple decades,
use a range. Only use null if the document provides no date information
whatsoever — this should be rare since research documents almost always
cite dated sources.

sources (array, at least 1 required)
------------------------------------
Each source object has:
  {
    "url": "https://...",
    "title": "Paper or article title",
    "source_type": "academic | journalism | book | social_media | government |
                    primary | industry_research | practitioner | other",
    "snippet": "..."
  }

snippet rules:
- 1-2 sentences from the source that directly support the claim
- DO NOT FABRICATE snippets. If the document does not contain a direct quote
  from the source, write a paraphrase prefixed with "[paraphrased]:" so the
  reviewer knows it is not verbatim
- Example: "[paraphrased]: Study found that 62% of subjects showed the effect
  under high-load conditions"

If the document cites a source but you cannot extract a meaningful snippet
or paraphrase, it is better to skip the entire finding than fabricate one.
Findings without any source must be excluded entirely.

url rules:
- If the document provides a URL, use it exactly
- If the document cites a paper without a URL, use the DOI URL format if
  the DOI is provided: "https://doi.org/10.xxxx/..."
- If no URL or DOI exists, use an empty string "" for the url field — do
  NOT fabricate specific IDs or URLs. An empty string is honest; a fabricated
  link is dangerous.

============================================================
OUTPUT CONSTRAINTS
============================================================

- Output is a JSON array: [ {finding1}, {finding2}, ... ]
- No wrapping object, no markdown code fences, no preamble
- Every finding must have ALL required fields (claim, elaboration,
  confidence_score, confidence_basis, root_anxieties, primary_circuits,
  academic_discipline, cultural_domains, era, sources)
- If you cannot populate a required field, SKIP the finding entirely
- The array is directly passed to json.loads() — any formatting error
  fails the entire extraction
